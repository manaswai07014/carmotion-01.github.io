In other words, cBP Director of Field Operations / XOverseas buyers of five luxury vehicles worth over half a million dollars are going to have to wait a bit longer for delivery. The vehicles were seized in Tampa, Florida, last month by U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officers before they could leave the port.

That is — on June 26, the Port of Tampa’s vehicle export lot looked like a high-end car dealership. The collection was heavy on Mercedes-Benz hardware, including three G-Class SUVs and a 2023 AMG SL55. A 2023 Chevrolet Corvette Stingray rounded out the shipment.

To put it simply, apparently, the paperwork didn't match the cars. The declared export values were $257,000 less than the actual value, authorities say. Federal law requires exporters to provide accurate ownership information and supporting documentation before a vehicle can be shipped overseas.

Breaking this down, vehicle theft often grabs headlines, but fraudulent export schemes are another challenge facing law enforcement. In this case, authorities did not indicate whether the vehicles had been reported stolen, only that the documentation accompanying the shipment was fraudulent.

Now, “Export fraud is not a paperwork violation—it is a serious crime that can conceal money laundering, sanctions evasion, and other transnational criminal activity,” said Director of Field Operations for CBP’s Miami and Tampa Field Office Daniel Alonso.
